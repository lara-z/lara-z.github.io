MODULAR, SENSORIZED ROBOTIC HANDS Aug. 2020 - Present

I performed this work at MIT under the supervision of Professors Wojciech Matusik and Pulkit Agrawal as part of my master's research. Jie Xu developed a computer program to assemble the components and assembly rules I created. Kui Wu and Yiyue Luo created a computer program to automatically generate knitted tactile sensors that correspond to the shape of the manipulators I designed.

This work was also used in the RSS publication.

Objective

Robotic hands are time-consuming to design from scratch and require extensive testing to produce a high-quality product. To streamline this process, I created a pipeline for designing and manufacturing cable-driven robotic manipulators with integrated tactile sensing. In addition, I designed a "Lego kit" of subcomponent 3D models and assembly instructions (grammar production rules) so that, given these subcomponents and assembly rules, a user can quickly combine subcomponents to create a custom functional, manufacturable, sensorized, and geometrically deformable manipulators. Once the robot manipulator is virtually assembled in the pipeline program, 3D printing and machine knitting files will be automatically generated to manufacture the robotic hand with knitted tactile sensing.

Modular design

I designed a "Lego kit" of virtual subcomponents that can be combined following a set of assembly "grammar production" rules to create manipulator hands of many topologies. The subcomponents are specially designed to maintain manufacturability after geometric deformation (the second step of the pipeline) and to effectively route cables in any of the almost infinite combinations of subcomponents. The production rules were chosen to ensure a manufacturable and functional manipulator design.

With only thirteen input components and twenty-two assembly rules, the design space is incredibly rich: it can create on the order of 1019 unique robotic manipulator topologies.

To create a manipulator, the user first applies the palm assembly rules on a computer to combine the palm subcomponents until they form a 3D model of their desired palm. Then, they apply the finger production rules to generate fingers on the palm. The result is a complete 3D model of the robotic hand ready for manufacturing.

Geometric deformation

No robotic manipulators to-date allow custom deformation before manufacturing. With my design, users can narrow, widen, lengthen, and shorten components of their robotic manipulator CAD to create a more optimally sized robotic hand.

The CAD of the manipulator can be intuitively re-shaped in a custom program while maintaining manufacturability

Building and testing

Using the design pipeline, I designed, manufactured, programmed, and tested four robotic hands: (1) an egg picker picks up, shakes, and puts down an egg; (2) a finger screws in a wing nut until tight; (3) a water bottle sorter grasps a water bottle, detects whether it is full or empty, then places it in a bin if empty or (if full) empties it then places it in the bin; and (4) a paper cutter uses any pair of office scissors to cut paper and will detect if a harder material is substituted.

3D models and manufactured manipulators created to test the design pipeline. The pink patches on the 3D models show sensing locations.

The robot hands produced by the pipeline are cable-driven and actuated by Dynamixel motors. I programmed the motor movement and control from scratch in Python. Due to time contraints, I implemented only based if-then style open loop control. The tasks successfully demonstrated the efficacy of the design pipeline.

The sensors consist of two knitted layers with orthogonally overlapping piezoresistive fibers. Sensing points occur where the fibers overlap and are read by a custom circuit and program developed by Wojciech Matusik's lab.
