HUMAN-POWERED SPEED VEHICLE May 2018 - Present

Objectives

Lead the design, testing, and manufacturing of a human-powered vehicle. Design an aluminum frame for the vehicle that will be safe at high speeds. Design an aerodynamic fairing shell for the vehicle. Manufacture the vehicle from scratch.

A sketch of an intermediate frame design

About the vehicle

As president of the Human Powered Vehicles Team, I am leading a team of 25 engineers in designing a human-powered vehicle (a bike) to achieve target speeds of 70 mph. I oversaw the large-scale design of this vehicle, coordinating the subteams and ensuring that the club has the necessary resources and opportunities available to complete their work. Since we had never designed aerodynamic components for such high speeds before (70 mph), I obtained the help of several professors (Professors Savas, Mäkiharju, and Alam) to learn about optimizing aerodynamic design and to learn about real-world methods to reduce drag once the vehicle has been designed. The vehicle will compete in the IHPVA World Human-Powered Speed Challenge this September. We designed the drivetrain, frame, and aerodynamic fairing from scratch.

The initial design process

We selected our rider at the beginning of our design process, enabling us to design the bike around the rider. To determine the geometric constraints of the bike (the optimal seat angle, pedal location, wheels, etc.), we created an adjustable wooden model that would allow us to alter the seat and pedal positions until the rider was finally comfortable.

Model to determine the placement of the seat, pedals, and wheels

Inside view of above model to determine vehicle geometry

Once we finalized that model, we re-created it on a 2D wooden bike that the rider could ride to double check our dimensions and test our initial drivetrain design.

Wooden prototype using the dimensions from the initial model

The frame

I led a team of five in the design, testing, and manufacturing of the vehicle frame. We selected an aluminum frame because it is easier to manufacture and can be simulated more realistically for safety tests than carbon fiber. To create the frame, we took the limiting measurements of the bike system in our wooden prototypes then created the frame around the rider's dimensions.

SolidWorks frame rendering of final design

Using the impulse momentum equations, I calculated the force that would act on the vehicle in different crash scenarios (top, front, and side for rolling). I used the results of these equations to determine the parameters for FEA analysis testing in SolidWorks. Selecting a factor of safety of 10, we determined that the model would pass the test if it exhibited no more than 0.1 inches around the rider's torso and head or no more than 2 inches at the cranks. These tests were performed in a worst-case scenario where the vehicle is driving without the fairing.

SolidWorks FEA displacements results for the frame loading tests

Before building the final frame in aluminum, we created a PVC pipe mock-up to ensure that the dimensions were correct.

PVC pipe mock-up model of final frame dimensions

Along the way, we also tested the idea of incorporating a universal joint in our sprocket movement during steering to prevent the chains from rubbing and derailing. After much research, testing, and calculating, we decided to remove the universal joint from our system because the smallest structurally sound joint system was too large to fit between the rider's knees and because the steering could be re-structured in a different way that would allow for a smaller but still acceptable steering range. In our idea, one chain would run from the pedals to an fixed, intermediate sprocket on one side of the joint, then another chain would run from a movable sprocket on the other side of the joint to the front wheel axle.

A test of the universal joint steering idea

About the vehicle design

Vehicle Design parameters

  • Should go at least 70 mph in 2.5 miles
  • Must be designed to dimensions of selected rider
  • Must cost less than $14,000

Vehicle Design highlights

  • Recumbent rider to minimize frontal surface area, reducing drag, increasing maximum speed
  • Large front wheel (and rider with long legs) selected for highest speed
  • Front wheel drive to reduce chain length and chance of chain slippage
  • Frame fitted with roll bar behind and over head to enhance safety and minimize rider collision with any of the frame geometry
  • Camera system mounted on bike to assist rider
  • 30° steering fork rake angle stability based on touring and cruising motorcycle rake angle
  • 950mm steering trail for stability based on typical motorcycle trail
  • Materials selected to minimize weight, maximizing speed
